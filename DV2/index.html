Global Unemployment Rate

This map illustrates global unemployment rates in 2024. The highest unemployment levels are concentrated in parts of Southern Africa, particularly Eswatini and South Africa. These patterns reflect ongoing regional challenges such as limited industrial diversification and less working opportunities. In contrast, countries in Asia and the Middle East record notably low unemployment rates. For example, Thailand, Viet Nam, and Japan maintain rates below 5%, reflecting strong labor demand and growing service sectors.

Global Unemployment Rates by Gender (2014–2024)

This chart compares male and female unemployment rates from 2014 to 2024 across 10 influential countries, highlighting global and regional variations in labor market equality. Overall, unemployment rates are continuously higher in nations like South Africa and Brazil, particularly for women, highlighting persistent issues with economic instability, inequality, and job access. Meanwhile, other countries such as China and Australia demonstrate stable employment trends, with narrow gender gaps. Overall, most countries' male unemployment rates remain slightly higher than female rates, underscoring the persistent global gender disparity in the workforce.

Global Unemployment Rates by Age Group (2014–2024)

This line chart shows global unemployment trends of 10 influential countries from 2014 to 2024, highlighting that the 15–24 age group consistently experiences the highest unemployment across all countries. One reason is that young adults in this age range often face challenges such as limited work experience, job availability, and competition with more experienced workers. The next highest unemployment is observed in the under-15 age group, while the 25+ age group has the lowest rates, benefiting from greater experience and more stability in the labor market. There is also a notable peak in 2020 for most countries, reflecting the economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Global Unemployment Rate by GDP (2014–2024)

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) represents the total economic output of a country, indicating the size and strength of its economy. This bubble chart shows that countries with larger GDPs, such as the United States, China, and Germany, tend to have lower unemployment rates, indicating that bigger economies can better absorb labor market shocks and provide diverse employment opportunities. Smaller GDP countries generally experience higher unemployment. For instance, the Africa region has a higher concentration of countries with elevated unemployment rates, reflecting challenges such as limited industrial diversification.

This stacked bar chart shows global unemployment rates by education level, highlighting how educational level impacts employment opportunities. Across most countries, people with lower education levels experience the highest unemployment rates, reflecting limited access to skilled jobs and fewer opportunities in the labor market. In contrast, people with intermediate and advanced education face lower unemployment rates, benefiting from higher skills, qualifications, and greater demand in the labor market.
