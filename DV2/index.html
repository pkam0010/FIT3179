Global Unemployment Rate

This map illustrates global unemployment rates in 2024. The highest unemployment levels are concentrated in parts of Southern Africa, particularly Eswatini and South Africa. These patterns reflect ongoing regional challenges such as limited industrial diversification and less working opportunities. In contrast, countries in Asia and the Middle East record notably low unemployment rates. For example, Thailand, Viet Nam, and Japan maintain rates below 5%, reflecting strong labor demand and growing service sectors.

Global Unemployment Rates by Gender (2014–2024)

This chart compares male and female unemployment rates from 2014 to 2024 across 10 influential countries, highlighting global and regional variations in labor market equality. Overall, unemployment rates are continuously higher in nations like South Africa and Brazil, particularly for women, highlighting persistent issues with economic instability, inequality, and job access. Meanwhile, other countries such as China and Australia demonstrate stable employment trends, with narrow gender gaps. Interestingly, across nearly all countries, male unemployment rates remain slightly higher than female rates, underscoring the persistent global gender disparity in the workforce.

Global Unemployment Rates by Age Group (2014–2024)

This line chart shows global unemployment trends from 2014 to 2024, highlighting that the 15–24 age group consistently experiences the highest unemployment across all countries. One reason is that young adults in this age range often face challenges such as limited work experience, job availability, and competition with more experienced workers. The next highest unemployment is observed in the under-15 age group, while the 25+ age group has the lowest rates, benefiting from greater experience and more stability in the labor market. There is also a notable peak in 2020 for most countries, reflecting the economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Global Unemployment Rate by GDP (2014–2024)

This bubble chart illustrates the relationship between GDP per capita and unemployment rate across countries from 2014 to 2024. Each circle represents a country, with its size proportional to population and color indicating region. The visualization helps identify patterns where nations with higher GDP tend to have lower unemployment, offering insights into the link between economic growth and labor market health.

This chart compares unemployment rates across education levels (Basic, Intermediate, and Advanced) for selected countries. It highlights how higher education levels generally correlate with lower unemployment, reflecting the influence of skill levels on employability.
